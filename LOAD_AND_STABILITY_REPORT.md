# Intrinsic Load and Concept Stability: Methodology and Rationale

**Learning Sequence Database (AIDB)**  
**Report Date:** February 2025

---

## Executive Summary

This report documents how two distinct dimensions—**intrinsic load** and **concept stability**—have been assigned to the 291 elements in the Victorian Numeracy Learning Sequence. These dimensions support curriculum design and instructional planning by distinguishing between (1) the working-memory demand of a task *during* learning, and (2) how much reiteration and retrieval practice a concept needs to consolidate over time. Both dimensions use a three-level scale (Low, Medium, High), with elements left unassigned (NULL) where evidence or clarity is insufficient.

---

## 1. Intrinsic Load

### 1.1 Theoretical Basis

**Cognitive Load Theory (CLT)** defines intrinsic cognitive load as the demand imposed by the *essential* complexity of the material—specifically, the number of information elements that must be processed *simultaneously* in working memory for the task to be understood (Sweller, 2010; Sweller et al., 2011). Element interactivity is the core mechanism: tasks with few interacting elements impose low load; tasks with many interacting elements impose high load.

Intrinsic load is distinct from extraneous load (caused by poor instructional design) and germane load (effort devoted to schema construction). It is determined by both the structure of the information and the learner’s prior knowledge—novices experience higher intrinsic load than experts for the same material.

### 1.2 Category Definitions

| Level | Definition | Typical Characteristics |
|-------|------------|-------------------------|
| **Low** | Single concept, minimal interacting elements, accessible to most students at this level | Concrete, foundational, few moving parts |
| **Medium** | Two or three interacting concepts, requires solid prior knowledge | Several representations or operations in play |
| **High** | Multiple interacting concepts, high element interactivity, demands strong prior knowledge | Abstract reasoning, many elements to coordinate |

### 1.3 Assignment Method

Assignments are made via pattern matching on element **titles** and **learning objectives**, using keywords and phrases aligned with CLT heuristics. The logic is applied in order: High patterns are checked first, then Medium, then Low. Elements that match no pattern are left NULL. A small set of manual overrides corrects clear misclassifications.

**High patterns** target: place value to 1000, teen/ty misconceptions, grid problems, counting to 1000, ordinal problems, and relationship concepts (e.g. days/weeks/months/years).

**Medium patterns** target: combining/partitioning with multiple representations, bar models, place value operations, fractions, number bonds, arrays, measurement attributes, and similar multi-element concepts.

**Low patterns** target: stable order, cardinality, subitising, fluency, notation, simple counting, shapes, data recording, and other single-concept or procedural elements.

## 2. Concept Stability

### 2.1 Theoretical Basis

**Concept stability** refers to how readily a concept consolidates in long-term memory and how much reiteration and retrieval practice it typically needs. This is separate from intrinsic load: a concept can have low working-memory demand *during* learning but still require many revisits to “stick.”

Research shows that:

- Foundational concepts (e.g. cardinality, one-to-one correspondence) are acquired gradually over extended periods (Wynn, 1992; Spaepen et al., 2018).
- Some misconceptions (e.g. teen/ty, place value) persist across years (Victorian Department of Education; NCBI).
- Fraction concepts are notoriously difficult and resistant to change (Siegler et al.; Natural Number Bias).
- The equals sign is often misinterpreted operationally rather than relationally, creating a barrier to algebra (Alibali et al.).
- Missing-part problems are the hardest type of number bond task (Math Coach’s Corner).
- Measurement concepts (conservation, unit iteration) develop slowly (NCETM).

### 2.2 Category Definitions

| Level | Definition | Typical Characteristics |
|-------|------------|-------------------------|
| **Low** | Concept consolidates relatively readily; minimal reiteration needed | Procedural, fluency once learned |
| **Medium** | Moderate reiteration and retrieval practice needed for consolidation | Developmental, multi-step |
| **High** | Concept is fragile; requires lots of revisiting and retrieval to consolidate | Protracted acquisition, persistent misconceptions |

### 2.3 Assignment Method

Assignments are based on research on consolidation, reiteration, and misconception persistence. Pattern matching uses titles and learning objectives. High patterns are checked first, then Medium, then Low. Elements that match no pattern are left NULL. A set of manual overrides handles edge cases. Elements that are too vague or generic (e.g. “Problems,” “Language,” “Situations”) are explicitly left blank.

**High patterns** target: cardinality, one-to-one correspondence, stable order, perceptual subitising; teen/ty misconceptions; place value structure (renaming, unitising); fractions (halves, quarters, eighths); equals sign; missing part; balancing.

**Medium patterns** target: number bonds, counting sequence, digits & words; combining/partitioning; fair share/grouping; addition/subtraction concepts; measurement; MAB; estimating; arrays; quotative/partitive.

**Low patterns** target: fluency, notation, instructions, procedural skills, movement, repetition.

## 3. Distinction Between the Two Dimensions

| Dimension | What It Measures | When It Matters |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|
| **Intrinsic load** | Working-memory demand during learning (element interactivity) | Moment of instruction; pacing, scaffolding |
| **Concept stability** | How much reiteration/retrieval is needed for consolidation | Over time; curriculum design, spiral, retrieval practice |

**Illustrative contrast:**

- **10101 (Stable order & one-to-one correspondence):**  
  - Intrinsic load: **Low** (few elements)  
  - Concept stability: **High** (protracted acquisition)  
  - Implication: Simple to process in one lesson, but needs many revisits across the year.

- **11004 (Bar model missing part to 10):**  
  - Intrinsic load: **Medium** (several elements)  
  - Concept stability: **High** (most difficult number bond type)  
  - Implication: Moderate demand in the moment, plus high need for repeated practice.

- **39043 (Fluency):**  
  - Intrinsic load: **Low** (procedural)  
  - Concept stability: **Low** (consolidates with practice)  
  - Implication: Low demand and relatively quick consolidation once accuracy is established.

## 4. Why This Is a Good Starting Point

### 4.1 Grounded in Research

Both dimensions are tied to established literature:

- **Intrinsic load:** Cognitive Load Theory and element interactivity (Sweller et al.).
- **Concept stability:** Work on cardinality (Wynn, Spaepen), place value and teen numbers (Vic Ed, NCBI), fractions (Siegler), equals sign (Alibali), number bonds (Math Coach), and measurement (NCETM).

### 4.2 Transparent and Revisable

- Pattern-based rules are explicit and documented in the assignment scripts.
- Manual overrides handle clear exceptions.
- Ambiguous elements are left NULL rather than guessed.

### 4.2 Supports Curriculum Design

- **Intrinsic load** helps with pacing, scaffolding, and splitting complex elements across lessons.
- **Concept stability** helps with spacing, retrieval practice, and spiral curriculum design.
- The combination highlights elements that are both complex and fragile (e.g. teen/ty, fractions).

### 4.3 Coverage and Gaps

- **Intrinsic load:** 283 assigned, 8 left blank (generic “Problems,” “Situations,” etc.).
- **Concept stability:** 264 assigned, 27 left blank (vague “Language,” “Application,” etc.).

Leaving uncertain cases blank keeps the system honest and flags where expert judgment is needed.
